Frequently asked questions

What should I check first when my laser isn't cutting through?
Focus is almost always the first thing to check. Even being 0.5mm off focus can significantly reduce cutting power. After focus, check that your lens is clean — a dirty lens scatters the beam and dramatically reduces effective power. Then check air assist, slow down your speed, and consider adding passes rather than just maxing out power.
Why does my laser cut better on one side of the bed than the other?
This is almost always a bed leveling issue. If the cutting surface isn't flat and level relative to the laser head, focus changes across the work area and cutting power becomes inconsistent. Check your bed height at multiple points and use a honeycomb bed or adjustable risers to level it.
Will increasing power fix a weak cut?
Not necessarily, and it can make things worse. Before increasing power, check focus and clean your lens — both have a bigger impact than you'd expect. Maxing out power increases heat, which causes more charring and can actually make cut quality worse. More passes at moderate power usually outperforms one pass at max power.
How often should I clean my laser lens?
For regular use, inspect the lens after every few hours of cutting — especially when cutting wood or MDF, which produce a lot of smoke and residue. Clean it whenever you see visible residue, cloudiness, or notice a drop in cutting performance. Use proper lens cleaning solution and lens tissue — never paper towels or household cleaners.
Why does my engraving look fine on one material but terrible on another?
Different materials absorb laser energy very differently. Wood grain, MDF density, acrylic color, and leather tanning method all affect how the laser interacts with the surface. Settings that work perfectly on basswood may burn MDF or barely mark hardwood. Always test on the exact material and batch you're using for a real job.
What causes double lines in laser engraving?
Double lines are almost always mechanical. The most common cause is loose or stretched belts — the gantry "bounces" slightly between scan passes, offsetting alternate lines. Check belt tension on both axes. The second most common cause is a scanning offset setting in your software (LightBurn calls this "Scanning Offset Adjust") that needs to be calibrated for your speed.
These tools provide diagnostic suggestions and general educational guidance. Always follow your laser manufacturer's instructions, use proper ventilation and fire safety practices, and test on scrap material before running a real job.
